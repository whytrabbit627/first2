## The Most Important Thing First

Good nutrition during pregnancy doesn't require a perfect diet. Your body is remarkably good at prioritising your baby's needs and pulling nutrients from your own stores when your diet falls short. The goal is to eat well most of the time, not perfectly all of the time — especially in the first trimester when nausea often makes "eating well" mean "eating anything at all."

## First Trimester: Survive and Supplement

For many people, the first trimester is dominated by nausea, food aversions, and exhaustion. Nutritional perfection is unrealistic. The most important thing you can do in the first trimester is:

1. Take a prenatal vitamin with methylfolate (or folic acid) and DHA
2. Eat whatever you can keep down
3. Stay hydrated, even if only with small sips throughout the day

Folate is especially critical in the first 12 weeks — it supports neural tube development, which happens before many people even know they're pregnant. If you weren't taking a prenatal before your positive test, start immediately and don't worry too much about what you missed.

Foods that often work when nothing else does: plain crackers, ginger biscuits, rice, plain pasta, banana, toast. Eating small amounts frequently (every 2 hours) helps many people manage nausea better than three large meals.

## Second Trimester: Building Blocks

For most people, nausea eases in the second trimester and appetite returns. This is the time to focus on:

**Protein:** Supports your baby's rapid growth and your expanding blood volume. Aim for 70–100g daily from a variety of sources: eggs, chicken, fish, legumes, dairy, nuts.

**Iron:** Pregnancy doubles your iron requirements. Good sources include red meat, poultry, fish, lentils, and fortified cereals. Pair plant-based iron with vitamin C (a glass of orange juice, a handful of strawberries) to improve absorption. Avoid pairing with calcium-rich foods, which inhibit absorption.

**Calcium:** Your baby's skeleton is developing rapidly. Dairy, fortified plant milks, tofu, sardines (with bones), and leafy greens all contribute. The recommended intake is around 1,000mg daily.

**Omega-3 fatty acids:** Critical for brain and eye development. Found in oily fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) and supplemental DHA. Most prenatal vitamins include some DHA; additional dietary sources are beneficial.

## Third Trimester: Fuelling the Final Push

Your calorie needs increase in the third trimester by roughly 300–450 calories above your pre-pregnancy baseline. This is genuinely not much — an extra small meal or a couple of snacks rather than significantly larger portions.

As baby takes up more space, large meals become uncomfortable for many people. Smaller, more frequent meals work better physically as well as for blood sugar stability.

**Vitamin K** becomes particularly relevant in the third trimester, as it supports the blood clotting that will matter during birth. Leafy greens, broccoli, and fermented foods are good sources.

**Magnesium** supports sleep, reduces leg cramps, and supports the muscle contractions of labour. Nuts, seeds, whole grains, and dark chocolate are good sources.

## Foods to Limit or Avoid

- **Listeria risk:** Raw or undercooked meat, unpasteurised dairy, refrigerated smoked seafood, deli meats unless heated until steaming
- **Mercury:** Limit high-mercury fish (shark, swordfish, tilefish, king mackerel) to avoid accumulation. Low-mercury fish (salmon, sardines, trout, light canned tuna) are safe and encouraged
- **Alcohol:** Current guidance is to avoid it entirely during pregnancy
- **High-dose caffeine:** Keep below 200mg daily (roughly one cup of coffee)
- **Raw eggs and undercooked eggs:** Avoid — food safety risk

## The Bottom Line

Eat a varied diet, take your prenatal vitamin, stay hydrated, and don't let the perfect be the enemy of the good. Most pregnancies produce healthy babies with reasonable attention to the basics above. If you have specific concerns about your diet, a session with a registered dietitian who specialises in pregnancy is worth the investment.
