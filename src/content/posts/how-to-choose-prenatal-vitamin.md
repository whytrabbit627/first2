## Why Prenatal Vitamins Aren't All the Same

The prenatal vitamin aisle is overwhelming, and prices vary enormously. The good news is that the differences that actually matter are fairly straightforward once you know what to look for. Most of the variation is in a handful of key ingredients — the rest is largely marketing.

## Folate: The Most Important Call

Folate (vitamin B9) is the most critical nutrient in a prenatal vitamin for early pregnancy. It reduces the risk of neural tube defects like spina bifida, and the neural tube develops in the first 28 days after conception — often before a pregnancy is confirmed.

The key choice: **folic acid vs. methylfolate.**

- **Folic acid** is the synthetic form found in most standard prenatal vitamins. The body converts it to the active form (5-MTHF) before it can be used.
- **Methylfolate** (5-MTHF) is the already-converted, bioavailable form. It's ready to use immediately.

Up to 40% of people have a genetic variant (MTHFR) that reduces their ability to convert folic acid efficiently. If you know you have this variant, or if you want to remove the variable, choosing a prenatal with methylfolate is worth the typically modest additional cost.

Both forms are effective for the majority of people. If budget is a concern, a standard folic acid prenatal from a reputable brand is appropriate. The recommended dose is 400–800mcg daily.

## DHA: Brain and Eye Development

DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) is an omega-3 fatty acid essential for baby's brain and eye development, particularly in the third trimester and early infancy. Many prenatal vitamins include DHA; many don't.

Look for a prenatal that includes at least 200mg of DHA, ideally 300–400mg. If your prenatal doesn't include it, a separate algae-based DHA supplement (preferred over fish oil for purity and sustainability) is worth adding.

## Iron: Form Matters for Tolerance

Iron is necessary for the dramatic increase in blood volume during pregnancy, and most people need more than they get from diet alone. Prenatal vitamins typically contain 27mg of iron — the recommended daily amount for pregnancy.

The form matters for how well you tolerate it:

- **Ferrous sulfate** is the most common form and well-absorbed, but causes digestive upset (nausea, constipation) in many people.
- **Ferrous bisglycinate** (iron glycinate) is gentler on the stomach and well-absorbed. Found in higher-end prenatals and worth seeking out if iron has previously caused you digestive issues.

If constipation is a concern (common in pregnancy regardless), increasing fibre and fluid intake helps. Some providers recommend a stool softener alongside iron supplementation.

## Other Ingredients to Check

**Choline:** Often called the "forgotten nutrient" of pregnancy. Supports brain development and is rarely included in adequate amounts in prenatal vitamins. Eggs are a rich dietary source; a separate choline supplement may be worth discussing with your provider.

**Vitamin D:** Many people are deficient, and requirements increase in pregnancy. Look for at least 400 IU; 1,000–2,000 IU is commonly recommended. Your provider can check your levels with a blood test.

**Iodine:** Supports thyroid function and baby's neurological development. Look for 150–220mcg. Most people who use iodised salt get adequate iodine; those who don't (including those on restricted sodium diets) should confirm their prenatal includes it.

**Calcium:** Most prenatal vitamins include only a small amount because calcium interferes with iron absorption when taken together. Get the bulk of your calcium from food or a separate supplement taken at a different time of day.

## When to Start

Ideally, before conception. Folate's critical window is the first month of pregnancy; starting your prenatal 3 months before you plan to conceive ensures you're covered. If you're already pregnant and haven't started, begin now — the benefit is ongoing throughout all trimesters.

## Bottom Line

A good prenatal vitamin has methylfolate (or folic acid if budget is tight), DHA (200–400mg), gentle iron (bisglycinate ideally), vitamin D, and iodine. You don't need the most expensive option on the shelf, but you do want to read the label. If you're unsure, bring your current prenatal to your next appointment and ask your OB or midwife to confirm it covers your needs.
