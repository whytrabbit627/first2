## What the Wonder Weeks Are

The Wonder Weeks originated as a book by Dutch researchers Frans Plooij and Hetty van de Rijt, based on longitudinal research on infant development. Their central finding: babies go through a series of predictable developmental leaps in the first 20 months of life — periods of intense neurological development during which the brain reorganises and gains new perceptual abilities.

During these leap periods, babies are typically more fussy, clingy, and wakeful than usual. They may feed more frequently, sleep less predictably, and want more contact. Parents experience these periods as regressions or crises — what happened to my settled baby?

The Wonder Weeks framework offers a different lens: this isn't a regression, it's development. Your baby is working hard on something big.

## The App

The Wonder Weeks app (available iOS and Android) tracks your baby's age from their due date (not birth date, which is important for premature babies) and predicts the upcoming leaps with a calendar view. Green periods are typically settled; grey periods are leaps.

For each leap, the app provides:
- What developmental change is occurring (perception of patterns, relationships, sequences, principles)
- What new abilities your baby may be developing or demonstrating
- What signs to look for that indicate the leap is ending
- Suggested activities and games suited to the new perceptual level

## The Practical Value

The value of the Wonder Weeks is not that it tells you anything is wrong or explains what to do differently — it's that it contextualises difficulty. When your previously settled 8-week-old suddenly won't be put down and is feeding constantly, checking the app and seeing you're in the middle of a leap doesn't fix the situation, but it changes how you approach it.

Knowing this is temporary, predictable, and developmentally meaningful makes it easier to lean in rather than fight it. Most parents who use the app during a leap find that the fussiness passes within the predicted window — which is genuinely reassuring when you're in the middle of it.

## Calibration: Due Date vs. Birth Date

The app calculates leap timing from the due date, not the birth date. This is important for premature babies, whose leaps will come later than calendar age would suggest. If your baby arrived early, set the due date rather than birth date as the reference point.

For babies born exactly on their due date, the distinction doesn't apply. For those born significantly early, it matters considerably.

## Honest Notes on the Science

The Wonder Weeks has been critiqued for overstating the predictability of its leap calendar and for some of the research limitations in the underlying work. The leap windows are approximate, not precise — some babies are clearly in a leap outside the predicted window, and some sail through periods that other babies find difficult.

Use it as a contextual guide rather than a precise forecast. The framework is useful even if the timing isn't exact for every baby.

## Bottom Line

The Wonder Weeks app is one of the most practically useful things to have on your phone in the first year. It won't always predict exactly what's happening, but on the days when your baby is suddenly very different and you don't know why, it often provides the most reassuring possible answer: "They're growing."
