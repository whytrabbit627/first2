## The Follow-Up That Was Needed

If *Expecting Better* taught you to read pregnancy advice critically, *Cribsheet* applies the same discipline to the most contested decisions of early parenthood. The first year of a child's life is saturated with conflicting advice, strong opinions, and enormous parental anxiety — and very little clear thinking about what the evidence actually says.

Emily Oster's approach: find the studies, evaluate their quality, present what they actually show, and be honest about the limits of what we know.

## Topics Covered

**Breastfeeding.** One of the most nuanced sections in the book. Oster looks carefully at the evidence for the claimed benefits of breastfeeding — and finds that many of the strongest claims (notably around IQ and long-term health outcomes) don't hold up as well as commonly stated when you account for confounding variables. She also examines the evidence that does exist and rates each claimed benefit by how well-supported it is. The conclusion: breastfeeding has real but more modest benefits than often claimed, and the impact of an unhappy, stressed, or struggling mother is also relevant to the equation.

**Sleep training.** The chapter most readers want to get to first. Oster reviews the evidence on various approaches — including cry-it-out, Ferber, and gentler methods — and finds that the evidence for long-term harm from sleep training is weak to nonexistent. She's clear that sleep training is a choice, not an obligation, but that parents who want to use it can do so without guilt about causing lasting damage.

**Childcare.** A substantial section on the evidence around different childcare arrangements — nannies, nurseries, family care — and what the research suggests about developmental outcomes. The findings are more nuanced than either "daycare is harmful" or "daycare is neutral" narratives suggest.

**Screen time.** Evidence-based treatment of the screen time research, which Oster finds to be far less alarming than the "screens rot children's brains" position often taken. Context, content type, and whether screens displace other activities matter significantly.

**Vaccines.** A brief but clear treatment of vaccine safety, with the kind of numerical transparency Oster brings to all topics. The evidence for vaccine safety is strong; she says so plainly.

## The Recurring Theme

Throughout *Cribsheet*, Oster returns to a consistent message: parenting decisions involve trade-offs between evidence (which is often imperfect), your family's values and circumstances, and your own wellbeing. The "best" choice is not always the same for every family, and that's not a failure of the data — it's an acknowledgement that different families have different constraints and priorities.

She's also unusually honest about the things we genuinely don't know, and about the limits of observational data in parenting research — where it's nearly impossible to separate the effect of, say, breastfeeding, from the characteristics of families who breastfeed.

## What It's Not

*Cribsheet* is not a how-to parenting book. It doesn't tell you how to sleep train, how to start solids, or how to establish a routine. It tells you what the evidence says about whether each of these things matters, and in what ways. You'll still need other resources for the practical implementation.

## Best Read Before Baby Arrives

Like *Expecting Better*, this is a book to read before you're in the middle of it — ideally in the third trimester — so you're not trying to absorb it at 3am when you're already enmeshed in the decisions it discusses.

## Bottom Line

*Cribsheet* is one of the most genuinely useful books you can read before your baby arrives. It won't tell you what to do, but it will help you think more clearly about the decisions you'll face — and give you the framework to evaluate the avalanche of advice coming your way with some appropriate scepticism.
