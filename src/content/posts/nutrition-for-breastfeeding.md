## The Most Reassuring Thing First

Your breast milk is remarkably resilient. The composition of human milk — its protein content, carbohydrate structure, immune factors — is largely consistent regardless of what the nursing parent eats. Your body prioritises milk production, drawing from your own stores if dietary intake is insufficient.

This matters because the postpartum period is hard enough without the added pressure of eating perfectly to protect your milk. The research is clear: significant malnutrition in the nursing parent can affect milk supply and some nutrient levels, but ordinary dietary variation doesn't.

## What Does Actually Matter

**Calories.** Breastfeeding burns an estimated 400–500 extra calories per day. Under-eating consistently can reduce milk supply. In a period when many new mothers are tempted to restrict calories for weight loss, this is worth knowing: eat enough. This is not the time for aggressive dieting.

**Hydration.** Breast milk is about 87% water. Dehydration reduces supply. The practical advice: keep a large water bottle within reach during feeds, as thirst often strikes mid-feed when you're sitting still. You don't need to force yourself to drink excessive amounts — drink to thirst, consistently.

**Vitamin D.** Breast milk is low in vitamin D, and this is a genuine concern. The AAP recommends that exclusively breastfed babies receive a vitamin D supplement (400 IU/day) starting shortly after birth. Increasing your own vitamin D intake helps your levels but doesn't reliably increase milk vitamin D to adequate concentrations — supplementing baby directly is the recommended approach.

**Iodine.** Iodine passes into breast milk and is important for baby's thyroid and neurological development. If you weren't getting adequate iodine during pregnancy (see the prenatal vitamin guide), continue to ensure your postpartum diet or supplement includes it.

**DHA.** Continues to support baby's brain development throughout infancy. If you eat oily fish 2–3 times per week, you're likely covered. If not, a DHA supplement (algae-based preferred) is worth continuing from pregnancy.

## The Myth of Foods to Avoid

You will hear or read that you should avoid garlic, broccoli, cabbage, dairy, and various other foods while breastfeeding because they'll cause gas or fussiness in your baby. The evidence for this is weak.

Some flavours from your diet (garlic in particular) do pass into breast milk and change its taste. Babies generally don't mind — and early exposure to flavour variety through breast milk may actually support acceptance of diverse foods later. True food allergies in breastfed babies (most commonly to dairy protein) are real but uncommon, and involve specific symptoms (bloody stool, persistent rash, severe reflux) rather than ordinary fussiness.

If you notice a consistent pattern — your baby is more unsettled hours after you eat a specific food — it's reasonable to experiment with eliminating it. But eliminating multiple food groups "just in case" isn't supported by evidence and adds unnecessary restriction to an already demanding period.

## Alcohol and Caffeine

**Alcohol:** The guidance is nuanced. A single standard drink, consumed immediately after a feed (not before), results in alcohol levels in milk that peak at around 30–60 minutes and clear as your blood alcohol clears. The practical approach most lactation consultants endorse: if you have a drink, wait 2–3 hours before the next feed, or feed before drinking. "Pump and dump" is not necessary to clear alcohol from milk — only time does that.

**Caffeine:** Most of your caffeine intake doesn't reach milk. The small percentage that does is cleared relatively quickly. The general guidance is to keep intake under 200–300mg daily (1–2 cups of coffee). Newborns metabolise caffeine slowly, so very high maternal intake could theoretically accumulate — another reason to stay within the general limit rather than exceed it.

## Bottom Line

Eat enough, drink enough, continue a prenatal vitamin or nursing-specific supplement, make sure baby gets vitamin D, and don't stress about the rest. The list of things you genuinely need to do for breastfeeding nutrition is shorter than you've probably been told.
