## What This Book Is About

*What to Expect the First Year* by Heidi Murkoff is the most widely read baby reference book in the United States — and for good reason. It covers baby's first twelve months month by month, giving you a roadmap for what to expect at each stage developmentally, physically, and practically.

Now in its fourth edition, it's been updated to reflect current AAP guidance on feeding, sleep safety, and development.

## How It's Organized

The book follows a monthly structure, with each chapter covering:
- **Physical development:** What your baby can typically do this month
- **What your baby may be doing:** Developmental milestones at different percentiles
- **Feeding:** Breast milk, formula, and introduction of solids (when relevant)
- **Sleep:** Norms and common challenges for this age
- **Health:** Common illnesses, symptoms to watch for, and when to call the doctor
- **Raising a happy, healthy baby:** Tips and encouragement for this stage

This structure makes it easy to use as a reference — you can skip to the current month when you have a question rather than reading linearly.

## What Makes It Useful

**Covers the full first year.** Many books focus on the newborn phase. This one stays relevant from birth through twelve months, covering the transition to solids, starting daycare, separation anxiety, first words, and first steps.

**Practical, not preachy.** Murkoff's tone is reassuring without being dismissive. The book acknowledges that parenting is messy and hard, and it respects parents' ability to make their own choices.

**Health section is thorough.** The symptom guide at the back covers over 100 common conditions, with clear guidance on when to call the pediatrician, when to go to the ER, and how to manage at home.

**Updated guidance.** The fourth edition incorporates current AAP recommendations on safe sleep, vaccine schedules, and allergen introduction.

## Honest Limitations

The book is comprehensive — which can occasionally feel overwhelming. Some parents find the "what if your baby isn't doing this yet" sections anxiety-inducing. If you're prone to worrying, dip into it when you have a specific question rather than reading the upcoming month in advance.

Development ranges are presented as what's typical, not what's required. If your baby is meeting their own milestones at their own pace, that's what matters.

## Who It's For

**New parents who want a reliable reference** they can consult when something comes up — a rash, a sleep regression, a feeding question. It's not a parenting philosophy book; it's a practical companion.

Pairs well with *Cribsheet* (Emily Oster's data-driven take on first-year decisions) and *Healthy Sleep Habits, Happy Child* (for a dedicated deep dive on sleep).

## Where to Find It

Widely available in bookstores and online. The fourth edition is the most current.
