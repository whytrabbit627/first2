## Why Running Is Different After Birth

Running is a high-impact activity that places significant load on the pelvic floor. After a vaginal birth — and even after a cesarean — the connective tissue, muscles, and ligaments that support the pelvis and core need time to recover before absorbing that impact.

The widely cited **Groom, Donnelly & Brockwell guidelines (2019)** recommend waiting until at least **12 weeks postpartum** before returning to running, and only then if specific milestones are met.

## Before You Run: The 12-Week Checklist

At 12 weeks, you should be able to do all of the following without pain, heaviness, leaking, or discomfort:

- Walk briskly for 30 minutes
- Single-leg balance for 10 seconds each side
- 20 single-leg calf raises
- 20 single-leg bridges
- Jog on the spot for 1 minute
- Jump on the spot

If any of those cause symptoms, your body isn't ready yet — and that's normal. Continue building strength first.

## See a Pelvic Floor PT First

Before returning to running, a **pelvic floor physiotherapist** assessment is strongly recommended. They can check for:

- Pelvic organ prolapse
- Diastasis recti (abdominal separation)
- Pelvic floor muscle coordination (both weakness and tension)

Many women who are cleared at their 6-week check are not yet ready for running — the standard 6-week appointment doesn't assess return-to-sport readiness.

## The Return-to-Run Protocol

Once you meet the readiness criteria, follow a gradual run/walk progression:

| Week | Session |
|---|---|
| 1 | 1 min run / 1 min walk × 10 (20 min total) |
| 2 | 2 min run / 1 min walk × 7 |
| 3 | 3 min run / 1 min walk × 5 |
| 4 | 5 min run / 1 min walk × 4 |
| 5–6 | Build toward continuous 20–30 min run |

Stop and reassess if you notice leaking, pelvic heaviness, pain, or pressure at any point.

## Warning Signs to Watch For

These are signs to stop running and consult a pelvic floor PT:

- Leaking urine or stool during or after runs
- Pelvic or hip pain
- A feeling of heaviness or "something falling out"
- Low back or pubic symphysis pain
- Abdominal coning or doming with exertion

## If You Had a C-Section

A cesarean is major abdominal surgery. The scar tissue and fascia need time to heal before impact exercise. The same 12-week guideline applies — sometimes longer depending on healing. Gentle scar tissue mobilization (once the wound has fully closed, typically 8+ weeks) can help restore sensation and movement.

## Practical Tips

- **Run without a pushchair initially** — the twisting and pushing mechanics add load when your form is already adapting
- **Invest in a supportive sports bra** — especially if breastfeeding
- **Run on soft surfaces** when possible in the early weeks
- **Hydrate extra** if breastfeeding, as milk production increases fluid needs

Progress should feel gradual. If you were running before pregnancy, you may be surprised how conservative this timeline feels — but protecting your pelvic floor now prevents years of dysfunction later.
