## The Difference That Matters

In the first week or two after birth, it's normal to feel a wide range of emotions — elation, grief, overwhelm, love, panic, exhaustion — sometimes within the same hour. This emotional volatility is so common it has a name: the baby blues. Up to 80% of new mothers experience it, and it's considered a normal hormonal adjustment rather than a mental health condition.

Postpartum depression (PPD) is different. It's more persistent, more disabling, and very treatable — but only if it's recognised and addressed. The challenge is that PPD can develop gradually out of the baby blues, making it hard to identify the point at which normal adjustment tips into something that needs clinical support.

## Baby Blues: What to Expect

Baby blues typically begin within the first few days after birth — often day 2 or 3 — and resolve within two weeks. They're driven by the dramatic hormonal shift that occurs when the placenta is delivered: oestrogen and progesterone levels drop sharply, and the hormones that support milk production begin to rise.

Symptoms include:
- Tearfulness with or without a clear reason
- Mood swings that feel unpredictable
- Irritability and impatience
- Anxiety and worry about the baby
- Difficulty sleeping even when baby sleeps (partly adrenaline, partly adjustment)
- Feeling overwhelmed

These symptoms, while distressing, typically lift on their own. Support — from a partner, family, or friends — rest, and the knowledge that this is temporary and normal all help.

## When It Becomes PPD

If symptoms persist beyond two weeks, worsen rather than improve, or are severe enough to significantly interfere with daily function, PPD is the more appropriate framework. PPD can also begin weeks after birth — sometimes as late as 6 months postpartum — so it's not only a "the blues didn't lift" situation.

PPD symptoms to watch for:
- Persistent sadness or emptiness that doesn't shift
- Inability to enjoy things you normally would, including time with your baby
- Significant anxiety or panic, especially about baby's health or your ability to care for them
- Intrusive thoughts (unwanted, distressing thoughts that feel contrary to your values)
- Feeling disconnected from baby — unable to bond
- Anger, irritability, or rage that feels disproportionate
- Withdrawing from partner, family, and friends
- Physical symptoms: changes in appetite, inability to sleep beyond baby's demands, exhaustion that doesn't improve

It's important to note that PPD is not a reflection of love for your baby, your fitness as a parent, or your personal strength. It is a medical condition with a biological basis, and it responds well to treatment — therapy, medication, or both.

## For Partners: Recognising It in Each Other

Partners play a critical role in identifying PPD — often before the affected parent recognises it themselves. Signs to watch for:

- Your partner seems to be struggling well beyond the first two weeks
- They're expressing feelings of being a bad mother, that baby would be better off without them, or that they're a burden
- They seem disconnected from baby in a way that concerns you
- Sleep deprivation seems to be affecting them disproportionately compared to you
- They're not eating, showering, or engaging with normal activities

If you're concerned, raise it gently and without judgment: "I've noticed you seem to be struggling more than usual — I want to make sure you're okay. Would you be open to talking to your midwife or GP?"

## Partners Get PPD Too

Paternal PPD affects an estimated 1 in 10 new fathers, with higher rates when their partner is also struggling. It tends to manifest as anger, withdrawal, excessive anxiety, or throwing themselves into work rather than the tearfulness and sadness more commonly associated with depression. If you're a partner reading this and you're struggling — this applies to you too.

## Getting Help

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is a validated 10-question screening tool often administered at postpartum appointments. It's worth being honest when you complete it. If your score suggests concern, your provider can discuss next steps — which typically include therapy, peer support, medication, or a combination.

PPD is treatable. Most people who receive appropriate support recover fully.

## Bottom Line

Baby blues are normal and temporary. If sadness, anxiety, or disconnection persist beyond two weeks, worsen over time, or feel severe — reach out to your midwife, GP, or a mental health professional. You don't need to wait until you're certain it's PPD. You can simply say "I'm struggling and it's not getting better" — that's enough.
