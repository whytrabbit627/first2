## Worry Is Built Into Pregnancy

From the moment you see a positive test, you're responsible for something you can't fully see, touch, or control. Worry during pregnancy is not irrational — it's a natural response to a high-stakes situation with genuine uncertainty. Some level of vigilance, of attending carefully to symptoms and your body's signals, is adaptive.

The question is where ordinary, manageable worry ends and anxiety that needs attention begins.

## What Distinguishes Anxiety from Worry

Worry is typically connected to specific, identifiable concerns: Will the anomaly scan be okay? Is the baby moving enough? How will we manage financially? Worry responds to reassurance — a good scan result, feeling movement, a plan — and moves on.

Anxiety is more pervasive. It tends to:
- Cycle back to the same fears despite reassurance
- Generate new concerns the moment old ones are addressed
- Affect sleep, concentration, and physical wellbeing
- Feel uncontrollable and exhausting
- Show up physically: racing heart, shallow breathing, muscle tension, nausea (beyond morning sickness)

Perinatal anxiety is at least as common as perinatal depression — affecting around 15–20% of pregnant people — and often goes undiagnosed because anxiety is more normalised and less obviously distressing to outside observers.

## Common Anxieties in Pregnancy

- Fear of miscarriage, particularly in the first trimester and after prior loss
- Health anxiety about symptoms and test results
- Fear of labour and birth (tokophobia ranges from mild to severe)
- Anxiety about becoming a parent — ability to cope, relationship changes, identity shift
- Worry about the baby's health and development
- Financial and practical anxiety about the upcoming change

Any of these can cross from manageable worry into clinically significant anxiety. The measure isn't the topic — it's how much it dominates your thinking, whether it responds to reassurance, and how much it's affecting your quality of life.

## Practical Management Strategies

**Breathing exercises.** Slow, diaphragmatic breathing directly activates the parasympathetic nervous system — your body's "rest and calm" mode. Box breathing (4 counts in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold) or extended exhale breathing (breathe in for 4, out for 6–8) are both effective. These work in the moment, during an anxiety spike.

**Scheduled worry time.** Rather than fighting intrusive worries throughout the day, set a 20-minute window each day for deliberate worrying. When a worry arises outside that window, note it and redirect it. This sounds clinical but genuinely reduces the infiltration of worry into the rest of your day.

**Movement.** Regular moderate exercise is one of the most effective evidence-based interventions for anxiety — comparable in effect size to low-dose medication for mild-moderate anxiety. Even a daily 30-minute walk shifts anxiety meaningfully over time.

**Reducing information overload.** Endless symptom-searching typically increases anxiety rather than resolving it. Setting boundaries on when and how much you engage with pregnancy forums, symptom checkers, and social media comparison is a concrete, controllable step.

**Talking to your provider.** Anxiety during pregnancy is a medical concern, not a character flaw. Your midwife or OB should know if you're struggling. There are effective treatments — therapy (particularly CBT and mindfulness-based approaches), and medications that are considered safe in pregnancy for cases where anxiety is severe.

## Therapy Options

**Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)** is the most evidence-supported therapy for anxiety. In a pregnancy context, it helps you identify the thought patterns driving anxiety (catastrophising, overestimating risk) and develop more balanced responses.

**Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)** combines mindfulness practices with CBT techniques and has strong evidence for anxiety reduction in the perinatal period.

Many therapists now offer sessions by video, which removes the logistical barrier of in-person attendance during pregnancy.

## Medication in Pregnancy

If anxiety is severe, medication may be appropriate and safe. This is a conversation to have with your provider, not to manage alone. Many common anxiety medications are considered low-risk in pregnancy; others are avoided. Your care team can help you weigh the risk of untreated anxiety (which is also a risk) against the specific medication in question.

## Bottom Line

Some worry in pregnancy is normal and doesn't need to be treated. But if anxiety is affecting your sleep, your relationships, your ability to function — or if it simply doesn't feel manageable — that's worth talking to someone about. Effective help exists, and you don't need to white-knuckle through pregnancy feeling this way.
