## Why Partner Nutrition Matters

The focus on nutrition in pregnancy is overwhelmingly directed at the pregnant person — and for good reason. But the conversation often stops there, leaving partners feeling like passive bystanders in a process that requires two people.

Partner nutrition matters in three distinct ways: it affects conception if you're still in the trying-to-conceive phase, it affects your resilience and availability as a support person throughout pregnancy, and it sets household patterns that will matter when a new baby arrives.

## Sperm Health: The Overlooked Variable

If you're still trying to conceive, or if conception took time, it's worth knowing that male-factor infertility accounts for around 40% of fertility challenges. Sperm health — count, motility, and morphology — is influenced by lifestyle factors including diet.

**Antioxidants** protect sperm from oxidative damage. Key sources:
- **Vitamin C:** Citrus, strawberries, bell peppers, broccoli
- **Vitamin E:** Nuts, seeds, vegetable oils, leafy greens
- **Zinc:** Shellfish (oysters are particularly rich), meat, seeds, legumes
- **Folate:** Yes, partners benefit from folate too — it supports sperm DNA integrity. Leafy greens, legumes, fortified grains
- **Selenium:** Brazil nuts (one or two a day is sufficient and you shouldn't exceed this), fish, meat

**Omega-3 fatty acids** support healthy sperm membrane composition. Oily fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), walnuts, and algae-based supplements all contribute.

Diet improvements typically take 2–3 months to affect sperm quality (the time for sperm to develop) — so changes made pre-conception are the most impactful.

## Energy for the Support Role

Pregnancy is a marathon, not a sprint — and so is supporting a pregnant partner. The physical and emotional demands of the third trimester and the newborn period are significant. Chronic sleep deprivation, stress, and poor nutrition compound each other in ways that affect your patience, your problem-solving, and your ability to be genuinely present.

This isn't about a dramatic dietary overhaul. It's about:

- Eating enough protein to maintain muscle and energy (most partners are fine here; athletes and those under high stress may need more)
- Maintaining blood sugar stability with regular meals rather than skipping and compensating
- Limiting alcohol, which degrades sleep quality even in moderate amounts — relevant when you're already sleep-deprived
- Reducing ultra-processed food in favour of whole foods that provide sustained energy

## The Household Diet Shift

One of the most practical things a partner can do is align their eating with the household. When the pregnant person is avoiding certain foods (alcohol, high-mercury fish, deli meats), choosing to avoid the same things normalises it and removes a potential point of tension.

As baby approaches and beyond, the household diet becomes increasingly important: the foods you keep in the house, the cooking patterns you establish, the snacks available at 3am — these all matter more with a newborn in the picture. Building better habits now pays dividends.

## Simple Priorities

Without making this a prescription:

1. Eat more oily fish (two servings per week is a good target)
2. Add a daily handful of nuts and seeds
3. Include leafy greens regularly
4. Reduce alcohol, particularly in the late pregnancy and newborn period
5. Eat breakfast — blood sugar stability in the morning affects energy and mood throughout the day

## Bottom Line

Partner nutrition doesn't need to be complicated or feel like a regime. Small, consistent improvements to what you eat support sperm health before conception, your resilience as a support person during pregnancy, and the household environment as you head into new parenthood.
