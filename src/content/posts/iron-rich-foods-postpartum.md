## Why Iron Matters After Birth

The average vaginal birth involves blood loss of around 500ml. A caesarean section typically involves 1,000ml or more. This blood loss depletes iron stores — and iron is what your red blood cells use to carry oxygen around your body.

The result of low iron (anaemia) is the kind of exhaustion that feels different from ordinary new-parent sleep deprivation: a deep, heavy fatigue that doesn't lift with rest, often accompanied by brain fog, shortness of breath on mild exertion, and pallor. For many postpartum women, this goes undiagnosed because the symptoms are attributed to the demands of new parenthood.

A blood test (haemoglobin and ferritin) at your postpartum check can confirm whether anaemia is contributing to how you feel.

## Haem vs. Non-Haem Iron

Not all dietary iron is absorbed equally. Understanding the two types helps you eat strategically:

**Haem iron** is found in animal products — meat, poultry, and fish. It's absorbed at roughly 15–35% efficiency, regardless of what you eat it with. Red meat and organ meats (particularly liver) are the richest sources.

**Non-haem iron** is found in plant-based sources — legumes, dark leafy greens, tofu, fortified cereals, nuts, and seeds. It's absorbed at a much lower rate (2–20%), but absorption can be significantly improved by pairing with vitamin C.

## Best Food Sources

**High haem iron:**
- Beef (especially lean cuts) — 2–3mg per 100g
- Liver (chicken or beef) — exceptionally rich, but limit to once a week due to high vitamin A content
- Oysters — among the richest sources per gram
- Sardines and tuna — good and practical options

**High non-haem iron:**
- Lentils — 3.3mg per 100g cooked
- Chickpeas and kidney beans — 2–3mg per 100g
- Firm tofu — around 3mg per 100g
- Spinach and dark leafy greens — 2–3mg per 100g (enhanced significantly by vitamin C pairing)
- Fortified breakfast cereals — check labels, varies widely
- Pumpkin seeds — good snack source

## Improving Absorption

**Pair with vitamin C.** A glass of orange juice, a handful of strawberries, red pepper, or kiwi eaten with an iron-rich meal can double or triple the absorption of non-haem iron.

**Avoid pairing with calcium.** Calcium competes with iron for absorption. Avoid eating high-calcium foods (milk, yoghurt, cheese) in the same meal as high-iron foods if you're trying to maximise absorption.

**Avoid tea and coffee near meals.** The tannins in tea and coffee inhibit iron absorption. A general rule: wait 30–60 minutes after a meal before having tea or coffee.

## When Food Isn't Enough

If your blood test shows low ferritin (stored iron), dietary changes alone may not be sufficient to restore levels quickly. Iron supplementation is often recommended — and the form matters for tolerability. Ferrous bisglycinate (iron glycinate) is gentler on the stomach than ferrous sulfate and still well-absorbed.

If your midwife or GP prescribes iron, take it consistently and as directed. Iron stores can take 3–6 months to fully replenish after a significant depletion.

## Bottom Line

Postpartum fatigue has multiple causes, but iron deficiency is one of the most common and most treatable. Ask for a blood test if you're feeling a heaviness that sleep isn't fixing. In the meantime, prioritising iron-rich foods and pairing them with vitamin C is a practical, low-effort step you can take immediately.
