## When Is My Baby Ready?

Most babies are ready for solid foods around **6 months**, though readiness matters more than age. Look for:

- Sitting up with minimal support
- Good head and neck control
- Showing interest in food (watching you eat, reaching for your plate)
- Loss of the tongue-thrust reflex (not automatically pushing food out with their tongue)

Don't start before 4 months. The AAP and WHO recommend exclusive breastfeeding or formula through 6 months if possible.

## Baby-Led Weaning (BLW)

Baby-led weaning skips puréed spoon-feeding entirely. You offer soft, appropriately sized pieces of real food and let your baby self-feed from the start.

**Benefits:**
- Builds fine motor skills and hand-eye coordination
- Encourages self-regulation of intake
- Baby eats what the family eats (with modifications)
- No separate meal prep once you're in a routine

**What to offer first:**
- Soft-cooked vegetables: broccoli florets, carrot sticks, green beans
- Ripe fruit: banana spears, avocado, soft pear
- Well-cooked scrambled eggs
- Toast strips with nut butter (if no allergy concerns)

**Safety:** Cut food into finger-length strips or large enough to grip but not swallow whole. Always supervise. Learn the difference between gagging (normal, protective reflex) and choking (silent, requires action).

## Purée-First Approach

Traditional spoon-feeding with smooth purées is equally valid. It gives you more control over texture progression and can feel more manageable for first-time parents.

**Progression:**
1. Single-ingredient thin purées (sweet potato, peas, applesauce)
2. Thicker purées with more texture
3. Mashed foods with soft lumps
4. Soft finger foods around 8–9 months

**Tips:** Introduce new foods one at a time over 3–5 days to watch for allergic reactions. Offer vegetables before fruit — no evidence it matters long-term, but some parents prefer it.

## The Combination Approach

Many families do both — spoon-feeding puréed foods while also offering soft finger foods alongside. This is perfectly fine and lets your baby develop both skills.

## Foods to Avoid in the First Year

- **Honey** — risk of infant botulism until 12 months
- **Cow's milk as main drink** — breast milk or formula remains primary until 12 months
- **Choking hazards** — whole grapes, raw carrots, nuts, popcorn, hard chunks
- **Added salt and sugar** — baby kidneys aren't ready for added salt
- **High-mercury fish** — swordfish, shark, king mackerel

## Iron Matters

Breast milk is low in iron after 6 months, so iron-rich foods are a priority: pureed meats, well-cooked lentils, iron-fortified cereals, and eggs. Pair with vitamin C sources to boost absorption.

## Milk Remains Primary

Solid foods at this age are about exploration and learning — **breast milk or formula remains the primary nutrition source through the first year**. Don't stress if most of the food ends up on the floor.
