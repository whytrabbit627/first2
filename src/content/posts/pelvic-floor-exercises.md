## More Than Kegels

The pelvic floor is a hammock of muscles spanning the base of the pelvis, supporting the bladder, bowel, and uterus. During pregnancy, it bears increasing weight as your baby grows. During vaginal birth, it stretches dramatically. Postpartum, it needs to both recover strength and maintain the flexibility to function well.

The common advice — do your Kegels — captures only half the picture. An overly tight pelvic floor can cause as many problems as a weak one: difficulty with penetrative sex, increased tearing during birth, and paradoxically, bladder urgency and leakage. Balanced pelvic floor function requires both strength and the ability to relax fully.

## Finding Your Pelvic Floor

Before strengthening, you need to find the right muscles. The classic instruction ("squeeze as if you're stopping the flow of urine") works for identification but shouldn't be practiced during actual urination.

A more useful cue: imagine you're trying to lift a blueberry off a chair with your perineum. Feel the lift and squeeze of the muscles around your vagina, urethra, and anus — all three should engage, not just the front.

**The relaxation is equally important.** After each contraction, consciously release the muscles to a full, open state. This is where many people miss the second half of the exercise: holding a partial contraction without fully letting go. Practice visualising the muscles softening, widening, and letting go completely.

## The Basic Exercises

### Slow Contractions

1. Find your pelvic floor muscles
2. Squeeze and lift, aiming for 30–40% of maximum effort (not a full, straining squeeze)
3. Hold for 10 seconds if you can, or as long as you can maintain without holding your breath
4. Release fully and rest for 10 seconds
5. Repeat 8–10 times

### Fast Contractions (Functional Training)

These train your pelvic floor to respond quickly to sudden pressure increases — the kind that happen with coughing, sneezing, and jumping.

1. Quickly contract your pelvic floor
2. Hold for 1 second
3. Release fully
4. Repeat 10 times

Both types of contraction are important. Fast contractions protect against stress incontinence; slow contractions build endurance and support.

### Breathing Coordination

Your pelvic floor and diaphragm work as a team. On the inhale, your diaphragm descends and your pelvic floor gently expands. On the exhale, both return. Practicing this coordination — consciously relaxing the pelvic floor on the inhale and gently lifting on the exhale — integrates pelvic floor function into your breathing rather than making it an isolated exercise.

## Pregnancy-Specific Considerations

**Start early.** The pelvic floor is most responsive to training before significant loading occurs. Starting in the first trimester and maintaining throughout is more effective than starting in the third.

**Don't overdo it.** More is not always better. 3–4 sets of 10 repetitions, 3–4 times per week, is adequate for most people. Excessive Kegels in an already-tight pelvic floor can worsen tension.

**Perineal massage.** From 34–35 weeks, regular perineal massage (stretching the tissue of the perineum) can reduce tearing and improve recovery time. Many midwives can demonstrate the technique, and guides are widely available online.

## Postpartum Recovery

After vaginal birth, wait until any significant perineal pain has settled (typically 3–6 weeks) before resuming pelvic floor exercises. Start gently — with awareness and very gentle contractions — before building intensity.

After caesarean section, the pelvic floor still needs attention. While it wasn't involved in birth, it bore nine months of pregnancy weight and needs to be rehabilitated alongside the abdominal muscles.

Return to impact exercise (running, jumping) should be gradual and guided by pelvic floor readiness, not just the passage of time. The standard "6-week clearance" for exercise does not mean your pelvic floor is ready for a 5km run.

## When to See a Pelvic Floor Physiotherapist

Consider a referral if you experience:
- Leaking urine with coughing, sneezing, or exercise
- Urgency (strong, sudden urge to urinate) or frequency
- Pelvic organ prolapse symptoms (a bulging sensation)
- Pain with penetrative sex (postpartum)
- Difficulty fully emptying your bladder or bowel

Many countries offer postnatal pelvic physiotherapy via the public health system; in others, it's available privately. It's worth the investment — a 45-minute appointment can identify issues that self-guided exercise doesn't resolve.

## Bottom Line

Pelvic floor health is one of the most practical and rewarding areas to invest in during pregnancy. Start early, prioritise relaxation as much as contraction, and don't hesitate to see a specialist if you have any symptoms. A strong, coordinated pelvic floor is one of the best things you can do for your long-term comfort and function.
