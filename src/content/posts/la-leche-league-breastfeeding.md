## What La Leche League Is

La Leche League International (LLLI) is a non-profit organisation founded in 1956 by a group of mothers who wanted to help other mothers breastfeed. For nearly 70 years, it has trained volunteer peer counsellors (called Leaders) and run local support groups where breastfeeding parents can get information, encouragement, and connection.

LLL is not a class in the conventional sense — there's no curriculum and no assessment. It's a community. Groups meet regularly (monthly for most, more frequently in urban areas), and attendance is informal: come when it's useful, skip when it isn't.

## Why Peer Support Works

Breastfeeding challenges are almost universal. The majority of parents who want to breastfeed experience difficulties in the first weeks — latch problems, engorgement, nipple pain, supply concerns, the relentlessness of cluster feeding. These challenges are often temporary and resolvable, but they're also intense in the moment.

Professional support (lactation consultants, midwives, health visitors) is valuable, but access is often limited, appointments are brief, and the relationship is clinical. Peer support — from someone who has been through it, navigated the same 3am panic, and come out the other side — is a different kind of help. It's available at odd hours, it's sustained, and it comes from credibility through experience rather than credential.

LLL Leaders are trained in breastfeeding management and are available between meetings. Many groups have a phone or message contact for urgent questions outside meeting times.

## Why to Connect During Pregnancy

The middle of a breastfeeding crisis is not the time to find your LLL group. By the time you're dealing with a painful latch at day 4 postpartum, already exhausted and in discomfort, trying to search for and attend a new group is prohibitively difficult.

Attending one or two meetings during the third trimester (from 36+ weeks) lets you:
- Meet the local Leaders and understand how your group works
- Connect with mothers at various stages of their breastfeeding journey
- Understand common first-week challenges before you encounter them
- Have the group already in your phone contacts when you need it

You don't need to have specific questions to attend. Listening to other mothers discuss their experiences is useful preparation in itself.

## What to Expect at a Meeting

LLL meetings are informal and typically run 1–2 hours. Each meeting usually has a topic for discussion, often chosen by the Leader based on common questions in the group, but conversation ranges widely. Babies are present (and breastfed during the meeting — normalising breastfeeding in public is a founding value of LLL). Children of various ages are welcome; it's not unusual for an older toddler to be present alongside a newborn.

Leaders present factual information and share personal experience. They refer to professional help (lactation consultants, doctors) when issues are beyond peer support.

## What LLL Is and Isn't

LLL is a peer support organisation with a strong pro-breastfeeding orientation. They will provide information and encouragement to continue breastfeeding, but should not be the primary resource for mothers who are experiencing significant physical issues (mastitis, tongue tie, significant supply problems) — those require professional assessment.

LLL's breastfeeding guidance is generally in line with current evidence and WHO/UNICEF guidance, but Leaders are peer counsellors rather than medical professionals. Use their support for information, encouragement, and connection; see your midwife or a certified lactation consultant (IBCLC) for clinical issues.

## Online Groups and the Helpline

For those without accessible local meetings, LLL has online groups and a moderated online community. The international helpline (accessible via the website) connects you with a Leader by phone. Many people find a combination — online for immediate questions, in-person for ongoing community — works well.

## Bottom Line

La Leche League is one of the most valuable resources available to breastfeeding parents, specifically because it offers sustained peer support from people who have direct experience. If you're planning to breastfeed, attend a meeting during pregnancy. Build the relationship before you need it — you'll be glad you did.
