## The Problem with the 6-Week Clearance

At the 6-week postpartum appointment, most providers will ask how you're feeling and, if you say you're okay, will tell you that you're cleared for exercise and sex. Many people interpret this as permission to return to everything they were doing before pregnancy.

It isn't. The 6-week appointment is designed to catch significant complications — infection, wound issues, haemorrhage, significant mental health crisis. It doesn't assess pelvic floor function, abdominal separation, or your body's readiness for high-impact activity. The "6-week clearance" is a medical check, not a fitness assessment.

Returning to running, HIIT, weightlifting, or high-impact classes at 6 weeks without a progressive build-up risks pelvic organ prolapse, worsening abdominal separation, and injuries to deconditioned tissues. This is not about being overly cautious — it's about the specific physiology of postpartum recovery.

## What's Actually Happening in the First 6 Weeks

In the first 6 weeks postpartum, your body is:
- Healing the uterus (the placental attachment site heals like a large internal wound)
- Recovering from perineal trauma if you had a vaginal birth
- Healing a caesarean incision through multiple tissue layers if you had a c-section
- Managing the hormonal transition of the postpartum period, including the effect of relaxin (the hormone that loosened your joints and ligaments during pregnancy, which remains elevated while breastfeeding)
- Running on dramatically reduced sleep

Low-impact activity during this period — gentle walking, breathing exercises, pelvic floor work — is appropriate and beneficial. High-impact activity is not.

## Checking for Diastasis Recti

Diastasis recti (abdominal separation) occurs when the connective tissue (linea alba) running down the centre of the abdomen stretches and widens during pregnancy. Around 60% of women have some degree of diastasis at 6 weeks postpartum; most resolve significantly by 6 months with appropriate rehabilitation.

**How to check:** Lie on your back with knees bent. Lift your head slightly (like a mini crunch). With your fingertips, feel along the midline of your abdomen from your sternum to your navel. You're checking for a gap and any "coning" or "doming" (a ridge of tissue that pokes upward instead of staying flat).

A gap of more than 2 finger-widths warrants assessment by a women's health physiotherapist before returning to abdominal exercises.

**What to avoid with diastasis:** Traditional crunches, sit-ups, and leg raises performed flat on your back. These load the abdominal wall in a way that worsens separation rather than supporting recovery.

## A Progressive Return Framework

**0–6 weeks:** Gentle walks of increasing duration. Pelvic floor awareness and gentle contractions from week 1–2. Breathing exercises and posture work. No high-impact, no heavy lifting (including heavy shopping bags or a full laundry basket), no abdominal exercises.

**6–12 weeks:** Introduce low-impact activity. Walking pace increases, adding hills. Stationary cycling. Swimming (once any perineal wounds are healed). Begin progressive abdominal rehabilitation focused on deep core muscles (transverse abdominis) rather than superficial crunches. Pilates and yoga designed for postnatal recovery.

**12 weeks onwards:** Begin rebuilding capacity toward your pre-pregnancy fitness. Walk-run intervals before returning to sustained running. Gradual introduction of resistance training, starting with lower weights than before pregnancy.

**Before returning to running or high-impact:** You should be able to walk briskly for 30 minutes without symptoms, and complete 10 single-leg calf raises and 10 single-leg bridges on each side without symptoms. If you experience any leaking, heaviness, pain, or prolapse symptoms during these tests, see a pelvic floor physiotherapist before progressing.

## Signs You're Progressing Too Fast

Back off if you experience:
- Leaking urine with exercise
- A heavy, dragging feeling in the pelvis (prolapse symptom)
- Pelvic pain or pain at the caesarean scar
- Pain at the symphysis pubis (front of pelvis)
- Significant muscle soreness beyond what's expected (your tissues are deconditioned — you'll get sore faster than before)

## The Role of a Women's Health Physiotherapist

A postnatal physiotherapy appointment at 6–8 weeks is genuinely valuable — it's the functional assessment that the 6-week medical check doesn't provide. A pelvic physiotherapist will assess your pelvic floor function, check for diastasis, evaluate your scar tissue if applicable, and give you a personalised return-to-activity plan.

In many countries this is available through the public health system; where it isn't, it's worth the private cost.

## Bottom Line

Your body has done something remarkable and it needs time to recover. A progressive return to exercise — starting slow, building systematically, watching for symptoms — will get you back to full activity more reliably than rushing and setting back your recovery. There's no prize for being the first person in your mother's group to run 5km at 8 weeks postpartum.
