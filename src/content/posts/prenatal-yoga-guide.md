## Why Prenatal Yoga Specifically?

Regular yoga is not the same as prenatal yoga. General yoga classes include poses and techniques that need to be modified or avoided during pregnancy — inversions, deep twists, strong abdominal work, lying flat on your back for extended periods. A qualified prenatal yoga teacher has specific training in pregnancy anatomy and knows what to modify, when, and why.

Beyond safety, prenatal yoga is structured around the specific needs of pregnant people: building the hip flexibility and endurance that supports comfortable labour positions, developing breath awareness and control for use during contractions, and providing community with other pregnant people — which research consistently shows improves psychological wellbeing during pregnancy.

## What Happens in a Class

**Opening and centering.** Most classes begin with a few minutes of breath awareness — noticing the quality of the breath, beginning to slow it. This is both practical (training the parasympathetic response) and a transition from daily busyness to present-moment awareness.

**Warm-up movement.** Gentle movement of the joints, particularly the spine, hips, and shoulders. Cat-cow (spinal flexion and extension on all fours), gentle neck rolls, shoulder circles.

**Standing poses.** Warrior poses, low lunges, and squats adapted for pregnancy build leg strength, hip opening, and balance. Props — blocks, bolsters, the wall — are used freely to accommodate the growing belly and changing centre of gravity.

**Hip opening.** A sustained focus in prenatal yoga. Wide-legged forward folds, pigeon variations, and supported butterfly poses target the hip rotators and inner thighs — the muscles that need to lengthen and open for labour.

**Breathwork.** Most classes include dedicated breathing practice: ujjayi breath (steady, audible breath through the nose), slow diaphragmatic breathing, and sometimes specific techniques for managing contraction intensity. Even if you choose not to use these techniques during labour, the practice of staying calm and breathing through discomfort is genuinely useful.

**Supported rest.** Class typically ends in a supported savasana (final relaxation) on the side, propped with blankets and bolsters. This is valuable restoration, not something to skip.

## Benefits with Evidence Behind Them

A review of prenatal yoga research published in the *Journal of Obstetrics and Gynaecology* found consistent evidence for:
- Reduced low back pain and pelvic girdle pain
- Improved sleep quality
- Reduced levels of perceived stress and anxiety
- Some evidence for reduced rates of preterm labour and improved neonatal outcomes

The breathwork component in particular is well-supported: controlled breathing demonstrably affects pain perception and emotional regulation, making it a genuinely practical tool for labour.

## Finding a Class

**What to look for in a teacher:** Specific prenatal yoga certification (Yoga Alliance's prenatal certification, or training through a recognized prenatal yoga programme). Not all yoga teachers who "do prenatal classes" have formal training — it's worth asking.

**In-person vs. online:** In-person classes offer community, which is genuinely valuable during pregnancy. Online classes offer flexibility and access for those who can't get to a studio. Both can be taught well. If in-person classes aren't accessible, YouTube has extensive free prenatal yoga content — Sarah Beth Yoga and Yoga with Adriene both have prenatal programmes worth exploring.

**Timing:** Most classes welcome pregnant people from the second trimester onwards. Some studios accept people from 12 weeks; others prefer to wait until 16 weeks. First trimester, when nausea and fatigue are often at their peak, can be a difficult time to establish a new practice — starting in the second trimester when energy returns is often more sustainable.

## What to Tell Your Teacher

At the start of class, tell the teacher your gestation and any relevant complications: low-lying placenta, pelvic girdle pain, any restrictions from your midwife or OB. A good teacher will modify your poses accordingly and won't require you to explain yourself.

## Bottom Line

Prenatal yoga is one of the most comprehensive single investments you can make in your pregnancy wellbeing — it addresses physical discomfort, mental health, birth preparation, and community simultaneously. Find a qualified teacher, start in the second trimester, and treat the breathing practice as seriously as the physical poses. It tends to be the part that pays dividends when it matters most.
