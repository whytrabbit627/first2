## The Part No One Talks About

The focus of postpartum mental health conversations is overwhelmingly on new mothers — for good reason. But the nearly complete absence of conversation about partners' mental health in the postpartum period has a cost: paternal postpartum depression and anxiety are common, genuinely distressing, and almost entirely unaddressed in most families.

Around 1 in 10 new fathers experience depression or anxiety in the postpartum period. That figure rises to 1 in 4 when their partner is also struggling with PPD. These are not small numbers.

## Why It Happens

The postpartum period creates conditions that are genuinely difficult, regardless of whether you gave birth:

**Sleep deprivation** is universal. The cognitive and emotional effects of sustained sleep disruption — reduced patience, impaired decision-making, emotional dysregulation — affect everyone in the house.

**Identity disruption.** The shift to parenthood changes your relationship, your social life, your priorities, your sense of self. This is significant even when it's positive, and it often arrives faster than it was anticipated.

**Relational distance.** Your partner is recovering physically and adjusting hormonally. If they're experiencing PPD, they may be emotionally unavailable in ways that feel like withdrawal. Sexual intimacy typically pauses. The relationship that existed pre-baby changes substantially in ways that take time to renegotiate.

**Pressure to hold it together.** Partners are often implicitly expected to function — to work, to support, to manage logistics — regardless of how they're doing internally. The cultural message that the partner's role is to support, not to need support, is both common and harmful.

**Anxiety about responsibility.** The weight of being responsible for a new person's survival is real and can trigger genuine anxiety — about safety, finances, ability to cope.

## What Paternal Depression Looks Like

It often doesn't look like the tearfulness and withdrawal typically associated with maternal PPD. Paternal depression more commonly manifests as:

- Irritability, short temper, anger that feels disproportionate
- Throwing yourself into work or other activities as a way to be absent
- Increased use of alcohol or substances
- Withdrawal from your partner and baby
- Feeling detached or like an outsider in your own family
- Physical symptoms: headaches, digestive problems, fatigue beyond sleep deprivation
- A flat, grey, low-grade feeling that doesn't lift

Many partners experiencing this don't recognise it as depression because it doesn't match the stereotype. They attribute it to stress, to tiredness, to the difficulty of the situation. Sometimes that's true. Sometimes it's more than that.

## When to Seek Help

A useful question: is this situational difficulty (hard circumstances that are producing hard feelings) or is it interfering significantly with your daily functioning, your relationship, or your ability to be present?

If you're regularly losing your temper in ways that frighten you, withdrawing from your family, using alcohol to cope, having thoughts of harming yourself, or simply feeling like you're not okay — those are signs to reach out.

## Where to Go

**Talk to your GP or family doctor.** They can screen for depression and anxiety and discuss treatment options. Effective treatments exist: therapy (CBT particularly), peer support, and medication when appropriate.

**Postpartum Support International (postpartum.net)** has specific resources and a support group for dads and partners. Their helpline (1-800-944-4773) is not just for mothers.

**Talk to your partner.** If you're both struggling, that conversation needs to happen — without it, each of you is trying to manage alone while also trying to support the other. It's a hard conversation that's also necessary.

## What Helps Day to Day

- Sleep, as much as can be managed — even partial nights matter
- Physical activity (not for performance, just movement — a daily walk works)
- Maintaining one connection to your pre-parenthood self: a friendship, a hobby, even briefly
- Saying out loud to someone you trust that you're struggling

## Bottom Line

New parenthood is hard for everyone. For some partners, it crosses a line into something that needs more than time. If that's where you are, you're not unusual, you're not failing, and help exists. The only version of this that goes badly is not saying anything.
