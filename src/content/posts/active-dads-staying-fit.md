## Why This Matters

Exercise is not a luxury when you're supporting a pregnant partner or a new baby — it's a genuine tool for managing the stress, sleep deprivation, and identity upheaval that comes with the transition to parenthood.

The evidence is clear: regular physical activity reduces anxiety and depression, improves sleep quality, sharpens cognitive function, and increases emotional resilience. All of these are resources you need in volume right now. Maintaining some level of physical activity is not selfishness — it's infrastructure.

The challenge is that exercise often gets deprioritised when life gets complicated. The birth of a first child coincides with a significant reduction in physical activity for many partners — at exactly the time when they need those benefits most.

## The Honest Constraints

Before the baby arrives, the constraints on exercise are primarily about time and energy during a busy, emotionally intense period. You're probably working, supporting your partner through pregnancy, preparing the home, processing a significant life change. Ambitious training programmes often collapse under this load.

After baby arrives, the constraints are more acute: sleep deprivation, unpredictable scheduling, the practical impossibility of being unavailable for extended periods in the early weeks.

Acknowledging these constraints is a prerequisite to finding realistic solutions. The goal is not to maintain your pre-pregnancy fitness level on exactly the same schedule. The goal is to maintain some consistent physical activity through an inherently disruptive period.

## Strategies That Actually Work

**Lower the minimum.** If a 60-minute gym session isn't happening, a 20-minute run is better than nothing. A walk with the pram counts. Lower the threshold for what "counts" as exercise so that the real sessions feel good and the shorter ones feel like something rather than failure.

**Morning exercise.** Before the household is fully awake and demands begin, exercise is most reliably available. This means an earlier alarm — which means earlier sleep. The discipline required to protect this time is real but worth it.

**Find activities that include baby.** Running with a pram is possible from around 6 months. Baby yoga classes or dad-and-baby fitness classes exist in many cities and solve the logistical problem of who is with the baby.

**Exercise with your partner.** In the second trimester in particular, walking together is often comfortable and enjoyable for a pregnant partner. It's connection time as well as physical activity. After baby arrives, walking with the pram together is often one of the first "normal" activities that becomes accessible.

**Home-based exercise.** Not going to a gym removes one logistical barrier. Resistance bands, bodyweight training, and short online programmes (15–25 minutes) can be done during nap time or before anyone else is up.

## The Guilt Question

Many partners feel guilty taking time for exercise when their partner is heavily pregnant or in the early weeks postpartum. This is worth examining.

The guilt often comes from a genuine place — awareness that your partner's physical demands are far greater, that their recovery time is constrained, that being absent (even briefly) adds to their load. These are real considerations.

The counterpoint: a partner who is consistently stressed, underslept, and physically sedentary is less helpful, less emotionally available, and more likely to experience mood and health problems of their own. Exercise isn't time away from your family — it's maintenance of your ability to function for your family.

The practical solution: communicate clearly about when exercise happens, make sure it doesn't fall at times that maximally inconvenience your partner, and reciprocate with explicit time for their own rest and recovery.

## The Postpartum Period

In the first 4–6 weeks postpartum, your partner's recovery needs to be the priority and the schedules may be genuinely chaotic. Be flexible during this period; don't expect to maintain your normal routine.

From around 6–8 weeks, as some semblance of pattern emerges, reintroduce exercise deliberately. One or two regular slots per week, communicated clearly and protected reliably, is far better than sporadic longer sessions that cause resentment.

## Bottom Line

Maintaining some physical activity during pregnancy and new parenthood is one of the most practically valuable things you can do for yourself and, by extension, for your family. Lower your expectations for volume and intensity during the most demanding phases, find formats that fit your actual life, and treat it as maintenance rather than performance.
