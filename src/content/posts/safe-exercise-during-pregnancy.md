## The Evidence Is Clear

Regular moderate exercise during pregnancy is not just safe for most people — it's actively beneficial. A substantial body of research shows that exercise during pregnancy:

- Reduces back and pelvic pain (among the most common pregnancy complaints)
- Improves sleep quality, particularly in the second and third trimesters
- Reduces gestational diabetes risk
- Reduces the risk of excessive gestational weight gain
- May shorten active labour and reduce the rate of caesarean delivery
- Supports faster postpartum recovery
- Reduces symptoms of prenatal depression and anxiety

The outdated advice to rest and avoid exertion during pregnancy is not supported by current evidence, except in specific medical circumstances (see contraindications below).

## What Changes at Each Trimester

### First Trimester

For most people who exercised before pregnancy, continuing your existing routine with minor modifications is appropriate. The first trimester is often the hardest for exercise not because of physical limitations but because of nausea, exhaustion, and morning sickness.

**Modifications:** Avoid overheating — don't exercise to the point of significant sweating in hot, humid conditions. Stay well-hydrated. Listen to fatigue as a real signal rather than something to push through.

**What works:** Walking, swimming, cycling (stationary is safer as belly grows), yoga, strength training. Most pre-pregnancy activities are fine through the first trimester.

### Second Trimester

For most people, energy returns in the second trimester and exercise becomes more enjoyable. The growing belly begins to affect balance and centre of gravity.

**Modifications:** Avoid lying flat on your back for extended periods after 20 weeks — the weight of the uterus can compress the vena cava and reduce blood flow. Exercises done on your back can be modified to an incline or side-lying position. Avoid contact sports and activities with fall risk (skiing, horse riding, aggressive team sports).

**What works:** Swimming and aqua fitness are excellent in pregnancy — water supports the belly weight and reduces joint impact. Pregnancy yoga and Pilates specifically adapted for pregnancy are widely available and valuable. Brisk walking remains excellent throughout.

### Third Trimester

The growing baby and the physical demands of late pregnancy make high-intensity exercise increasingly challenging. This isn't failure — it's physics.

**Modifications:** Lower intensity, more rest between sets. Pelvic girdle pain (PGP/SPD) affects many people in the third trimester and may require avoiding movements that load the pelvis asymmetrically (like wide-stance squats, lunges on uneven terrain, or single-leg movements). A pelvic physiotherapist can advise on modifications if PGP is an issue.

**What works:** Swimming, walking, prenatal yoga, gentle strength work. The goal in the third trimester is maintaining activity rather than building fitness.

## Warning Signs — Stop and Contact Your Provider

Stop exercising and call your midwife or OB if you experience:
- Vaginal bleeding or amniotic fluid leakage
- Significant shortness of breath before you've exerted yourself
- Chest pain or palpitations
- Dizziness or feeling faint
- Calf pain or swelling (could indicate clot)
- Decreased fetal movement

These symptoms are uncommon during exercise in a healthy pregnancy, but they need assessment if they occur.

## Who Should Check With Their Provider First

Most people with healthy, uncomplicated pregnancies can continue or start moderate exercise without specific medical clearance. You should discuss exercise with your provider if you have:
- Preeclampsia or pregnancy-induced hypertension
- Placenta praevia (low-lying placenta)
- Preterm labour risk
- Multiple pregnancy (twins, triplets)
- Severe anaemia
- Significant cardiac or respiratory conditions

## Starting Exercise in Pregnancy If You Weren't Active Before

Pregnancy is actually a good time to establish exercise habits, if you weren't active before. Start with walking — 15–20 minutes per day, building gradually. Prenatal yoga classes are designed to be accessible to beginners. The goal is regular, moderate activity rather than intensity.

## Bottom Line

Exercise during pregnancy is safe, beneficial, and worth maintaining. Modify based on your trimester and how you feel, avoid overheating and fall risk activities, watch for warning signs, and talk to your provider if you have specific concerns. A moving pregnancy is a healthier pregnancy for most people.
