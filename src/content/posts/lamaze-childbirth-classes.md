## What Lamaze Actually Is

Lamaze has a reputation problem. Many people think of it as "the class where you learn to breathe through contractions" — a technique that feels slightly dated and insufficient for the actual experience of labour. This reputation is based on what Lamaze was decades ago, not what it is now.

Modern Lamaze education is comprehensive birth preparation. Lamaze International trains and certifies childbirth educators (CBCEs) in an evidence-based curriculum that covers far more than breathing: the physiology of labour, pain management options from non-medical to epidural, positioning and movement in labour, the hospital environment, intervention decision-making, and the immediate postpartum period.

The breathing techniques remain a component — they're genuinely useful — but they're part of a much broader preparation rather than the whole show.

## What a Lamaze Class Covers

The specific content varies by educator, but a comprehensive Lamaze class typically includes:

**The physiology of labour.** How contractions work, what the cervix is doing during effacement and dilation, how labour progresses through its stages, and the wide range of normal. Understanding what's happening mechanically makes the experience less frightening and the decisions clearer.

**Comfort and coping strategies.** Non-medical approaches to managing contraction pain: breathing techniques, position changes, movement, hydrotherapy (shower or bath), counterpressure (partner technique), heat, the birthing ball, and the shower. These are practised in class so they're available in the moment without requiring thought.

**Pain management options.** A balanced presentation of all options — epidural anaesthesia, opioid analgesia, nitrous oxide (gas and air), TENS — covering how each works, typical experience, potential side effects, and how it affects labour and the baby. The goal is informed choice, not a recommendation.

**Interventions and decision-making.** What continuous fetal monitoring, IV access, augmentation with Pitocin/syntocinon, and assisted delivery (forceps, vacuum) are, when they're recommended, and how to ask questions before agreeing. The BRAIN framework (Benefits, Risks, Alternatives, Intuition, Nothing) is typically taught as a tool for real-time decision-making.

**Partner's role.** How to provide physical and emotional support through each stage of labour, including specific techniques that partners can practise in class. See also: *The Birth Partner* by Penny Simkin for a book-length treatment.

**Pushing and birth.** Positions for pushing (not everyone needs to push on their back), the importance of following your body's cues, episiotomy vs. spontaneous tearing, and immediate post-birth procedures.

**Immediate newborn care.** The first hour after birth — skin-to-skin contact, delayed cord clamping, newborn assessments, early breastfeeding.

## Finding a Class

Lamaze International's website (lamaze.org) has a certified educator finder by zip code or postal code. Classes are typically offered at hospitals, birth centres, and by independent educators in private settings.

Hospital-based classes have the advantage of familiarising you with the specific policies and environment of where you'll give birth; independent educators often offer smaller classes with more personal attention.

The timing: most families do their birth preparation class in the third trimester, typically 8–12 weeks before their due date. Too early and the information feels abstract; too close to the due date and fatigue can interfere with engagement.

## Online Options

Several Lamaze-certified educators offer online classes, which is useful for those who can't access in-person options. The interactive components (position practice, partner techniques) are more limited online, but the content is equivalent. Worth supplementing with an in-person infant CPR class regardless.

## Hospital Childbirth Education vs. Lamaze

Many hospitals offer their own childbirth education classes. These are worth taking for the facility familiarity, but the content may be more superficial than a dedicated Lamaze course. For comprehensive birth preparation, a dedicated course is preferable — or both if you have the time.

## Bottom Line

A comprehensive childbirth class is one of the most valuable investments you can make in the third trimester. Understanding what's happening, having practised coping techniques, and knowing how to ask questions in the moment dramatically changes the birth experience. Lamaze's evidence-based, non-prescriptive approach makes it a solid choice for most families.
